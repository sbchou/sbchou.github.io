A Markov chain is a type of mathematical system.

It's used for all sort of statistical models of real-world processes:

from physics to chemistry and language detection.

Although the applications can be very complex,

The concept is actually quite simple!

Imagine a big pond filled with lily pads in a sunny park.

The pond is big but there are a countable (discrete) number of lily pads (states).

Now imagine you are a small frog in this big pond:

You start on one lily pad (start state) at the beginning of the day.

It's a nice day and you are motivated only by bugs and sunshine!

When you’re on one lily pad, you only think of the next one to jump to,

and you don’t really care about what lily pads you’ve jumped on in the past.

You are a frog.

We call this the memoryless assumption.

Your movements are predicted by transition probabilites between one lily pad and the next.

That's all there is to a simple Markov Chain!

Markov Chains might be simple, but they are simply fundamental.

They’re useful whenever we have a chain of events, or a discrete set of possible states.

Can you think of processes that Markov Chains might explain well?
